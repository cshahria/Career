I actually think this interview is more straightforward than some of the senior Product Manager and Business Systems Analyst interviews we've prepared for.

Based on the job description and your resume, your biggest risk is not being underqualified—it's convincing them that you'll genuinely enjoy the work and won't leave after a month. The technical requirements are very simple compared to your background. 

## What they're looking for

They're trying to answer questions like:

* Will this person reliably show up?
* Can they follow instructions exactly?
* Are they physically comfortable moving around for an entire shift?
* Will they pay attention to detail?
* Can they identify problems and communicate them clearly?
* Will they be easy to work with?

Notice what's not on that list.

They aren't looking for:

* AI engineers
* Product Managers
* Architects
* ServiceNow experts

They need dependable people who can generate high-quality training data.

---

# The positive story

I would avoid saying:

> "I'm just taking this until I find something better."

Instead, tell a story that is completely truthful but emphasizes why this role actually interests you.

Something like this:

> I've spent most of my career supporting enterprise technology and AI-enabled products. While my background has been largely analytical and operational, this opportunity gives me exposure to another side of AI—how high-quality training data is actually created. I enjoy learning new technology, staying active, and contributing to innovative projects. At this point I'm looking for meaningful work where I can contribute immediately while continuing to broaden my experience.

That sounds much stronger than saying you're "between jobs."

---

# If they ask:

## "Why are you interested in this position?"

I'd answer something like:

> My career has been centered around technology, operations, and AI-enabled products. At ServiceNow I worked with AI initiatives from a product perspective, but this role offers an opportunity to contribute at the foundation level where quality data is created. I enjoy learning new technology, I'm detail-oriented, and I appreciate hands-on work. I'm also local to San Jose, comfortable working onsite, and available for the shifts you're hiring for.

---

## "You seem overqualified."

This will almost certainly come up.

Don't argue.

Agree with them.

Then redirect.

Try something like:

> I understand why it may look that way. My previous roles were certainly more senior. However, I'm intentionally looking for an opportunity where I can stay engaged, continue working with emerging AI technology, and contribute without necessarily having management responsibilities. I don't view every position as needing to be a step up in title. Right now I'm focused on doing good work with a strong team.

That removes their biggest concern.

---

## "Won't you leave as soon as another opportunity comes along?"

Probably the hardest question.

A good answer:

> I understand the concern. I'm looking for stability just as much as employers are looking for reliable employees. If we both find it's a good fit, I'd like to stay and contribute. I wouldn't have applied if I wasn't genuinely interested in the work.

Notice you don't promise anything unrealistic.

---

# They may ask

> Tell me about yourself.

Keep it around two minutes.

> I have a background in business systems analysis, product management, and IT operations, primarily supporting large organizations in healthcare, financial services, and enterprise software. Most recently I worked at ServiceNow helping improve operational processes and AI-enabled workflows, partnering with engineering and product teams to solve business problems. Before that I worked in IT change management at First Republic Bank and in several ServiceNow implementation roles. Throughout my career I've enjoyed technology, process improvement, and working with cross-functional teams. What attracted me to this opportunity is that it combines technology with hands-on work in AI and robotics. I'm excited about learning something different while contributing to a project that's helping shape the next generation of AI systems.

---

# Questions about the work

They'll probably ask something like:

> Can you stand and move for eight hours?

Easy answer.

> Yes. I'm comfortable being active throughout the day. I understand this role involves repeated movements, following defined procedures, and wearing motion capture equipment, and I'm comfortable with that.

---

> Are you comfortable wearing VR equipment?

> Yes. I've worked around technology throughout my career, so wearing sensors or VR equipment doesn't concern me. I'd simply follow the procedures and make sure the data collection is accurate.

---

> Tell me about your attention to detail.

This is where your resume actually helps.

Instead of talking about Product Management, use your Change Management experience.

For example:

> At First Republic Bank I reviewed more than 1,200 infrastructure change records each month. Accuracy was critical because errors could impact production systems. I became very comfortable following procedures, checking details carefully, identifying issues, and communicating findings clearly. 

That directly translates to this job.

---

# Strengths that actually matter

I'd emphasize these five.

* Reliability
* Attention to detail
* Ability to follow procedures
* Strong communication
* Interest in AI technology

Those are exactly what the job description emphasizes. 

---

# Don't oversell your AI experience

One temptation is to spend ten minutes discussing Agentic AI.

Don't.

Instead say:

> My previous work exposed me to AI from the software and product side. What interests me here is seeing how the training data is created. Every successful AI system starts with quality data, so I think it'll be fascinating to contribute to that process.

That demonstrates enthusiasm without making it sound like the work is beneath you.

---

# Questions you should ask them

Toward the end of the interview, consider asking:

1. "How is success measured during the first month?"

2. "How long does the training typically take before someone is fully independent?"

3. "What qualities separate your top performers?"

4. "What kinds of robots or AI systems are these motion datasets ultimately supporting?" (If they're able to share.)

5. "What does the contract-to-hire decision usually depend on?"

These questions show genuine curiosity about the work rather than just the paycheck.

## My overall assessment

I think you have a very good chance of getting this position if you focus on fit rather than credentials.

Your resume demonstrates years of following structured processes, documenting accurately, identifying issues, collaborating with teams, and working with advanced technology. Those are valuable strengths for this role. 

The key is to make the interviewer feel comfortable that you're applying because you're genuinely interested in the opportunity, not because you're reluctantly settling for it while waiting for something else. If you communicate curiosity, reliability, and enthusiasm for contributing to AI development, your background becomes an asset rather than a concern.
